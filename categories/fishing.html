What You Can Catch

Common species from the beach and close inshore at Pāpāmoa
🐟

Snapper

Pagrus auratus
The main target for Pāpāmoa surfcasters. They patrol behind the offshore sandbars, especially around dawn, dusk, and tide changes. Best on low tide around dark. Cast past the first breaker and use tuatua, mullet, or pilchard for bait.
Min 30cmLimit: 7/dayYear-round
🐠

Gurnard

Chelidonichthys kumu
A reliable and excellent eating fish from Pāpāmoa Beach. Often caught alongside snapper. If crabs are stripping your bait, move along the beach. If crabs are not a problem, gurnard (and snapper) are likely around. No minimum size applies.
No min sizeCombined 20 bagGreat table fish
🦈

Kahawai

Arripis trutta
Frequently the first fish to hit your line at Pāpāmoa. They feed in close, often in schools, and put up a strong fight. Great for smoking. In summer, watch for work-ups just off the beach that bring kahawai within casting range. No minimum size.
No min sizeCombined 20 bagGood on lures
🐡

Trevally

Pseudocaranx dentex
Present along the Pāpāmoa coast, particularly during warmer months. They sometimes come within casting range when feeding around work-ups close to shore. More commonly caught from boats or kontikis. Good sport fish with decent eating.
Min 25cmCombined 20 bagSummer peak
🐢

Tarakihi

Nemadactylus macropterus
Primarily a boat species from the reefs a few miles offshore, but included here because Tauranga charter boats target them heavily. Outstanding table fish. Local charter operators fish them on flasher rigs at 80m+ depth. Well worth a charter trip.
Min 25cmCombined 20 bagCharter target
🦐

Tuatua (Shellfish/Bait)

Paphies subtriangulata
Pāpāmoa Beach is known for its tuatua beds. Collect them at low tide by feeling for shells with your feet in the shallows. They are one of the top baits for surfcasting here and have been used by locals for generations. Daily limit of 50 per person.
Limit: 50/dayTop baitLow tide collection

Surfcasting Tips for Pāpāmoa

Local knowledge from experienced Pāpāmoa surfcasters
01

Fish the Low Tide Around Dawn or Dusk

The most productive sessions at Pāpāmoa tend to be on the low tide around first light or last light. Find a spot where the offshore bar is closest to shore, then cast past it. Snapper patrol behind the bar and are most active during these transition periods.

02

Let the Crabs Tell You Where to Fish

If crabs are stripping your bait immediately, there are no snapper or gurnard in the area. Move along the beach until the crabs stop being a problem. When predator fish are present, crabs bury themselves. This trick alone will save you hours of wasted effort.

03

Get Your Bait Past the Breakers

Pāpāmoa fishing is best done with proper surfcasting tackle. A 4oz breakout sinker will hold bottom, and you need enough rod to clear the first or second breaker. The fish sit in the deeper water behind the sandbar. If you cannot reach past it, consider getting wet and wading out to improve your casting position.

04

Use Fresh Local Bait

Tuatua collected from the beach at low tide is one of the best baits for Pāpāmoa. Mullet strips, pilchard, and squid also work well. If you do not need bait string to keep the bait on the hook, that is a good sign. Local wisdom says if the bait string is the only thing stopping your bait from falling off, the fish simply are not around yet.

05

Watch for Work-ups in Summer

During warmer months, keep an eye on the water just off the beach for diving birds and surface splashes. These work-ups indicate kahawai and sometimes trevally feeding in close. If you can reach them with a lure or softbait, the action can be fast and furious.

06

Know the Kontiki Rules

Kontiki and torpedo longline fishing is popular along Pāpāmoa, but restrictions apply. Between 15 December and 15 February, kontiki gear is not allowed between 10am and 5pm in lifeguard-patrolled zones. You must also stay at least 300 metres from flagged swim areas when deploying any longline gear.
